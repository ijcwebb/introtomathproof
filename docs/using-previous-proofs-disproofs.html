Chapter 11 Using previous proofs / disproofs

Noticing that a conjecture can be proved using previous work you’ve done can save you a lot of time! Aside from that, it can often show you the way that future conjectures can be proved.

As conjecture is known to be , we know that every prime \(p\), \(p \neq 2, 3\), can be written in the form \[\begin{align*} p &= 6k + 1 \intertext{or} p &= 6k - 1 \intertext{with $k \in \mathbb Z$.} \intertext{Let's deal with these one-by-one.} \intertext{If} p &= 6k + 1 \intertext{then doubling it and adding two gives} 2p + 2 &= 2(6k + 1) + 2 \\ &= 12p + 2 + 2 \\ &= 12p + 4 \\ &= 4(3p + 1) \intertext{which is a multiple of 4.} \intertext{Similarly, if} p &= 6k - 1 \intertext{then doubling it and adding two gives} 2p + 2 &= 2(6k - 1) + 2 \\ &= 12p - 2 + 2 \\ &= 12p \\ &= 4(3p) \intertext{which is a multiple of 4.} \intertext{As noted in the first line of the proof, this proof covers all primes \textit{except} 2 and 3. However, conjecture \textbf{11.2} covers all primes except 2, so we should also check that the conjecture holds for 3. \newline \newline If $p=3$, then $2p+2 = 2 \cdot 3 + 2 = 6 + 2 = 8$, which is a multiple of 4. \newline \newline Therefore the conjecture is \textsc{true}. \hspace{8cm} $\square$} \end{align*}\]
